About the Holiday

Introduction

What is Sukkot?

מהו חג הסוכות

Hebrew Date: 15th of Tishrei — begins five days after Yom Kippur

Upcoming Date (2026): Begins evening of Friday, September 25, 2026

Meaning: "Booths" or "Tabernacles"

Liturgical Name: Z'man Simchateinu — The Season of Our Rejoicing

Sukkot is one of the Shalosh Regalim — the Three Pilgrimage Festivals (alongside Passover and Shavuot). Coming just five days after the intense, solemn fast of Yom Kippur, Sukkot is a dramatic shift in tone: it is universally characterized by profound joy. Like the other pilgrimage festivals, it carries dual significance:

Historical: It commemorates the 40 years the Israelites spent wandering the desert after the Exodus, living in temporary shelters and relying entirely on God for protection and sustenance.

Agricultural: It is also known as Chag HaAsif (The Festival of Ingathering), marking the end of the harvest in the Land of Israel when farmers gathered the last of their summer crops before the winter rains.
The Story

Historical Origins

The Wilderness Journey & Clouds of Glory

הָעַנָנִים וְהַסֻּכּוֹת בַּמִּדְבָּר

According to the Book of Leviticus (23:42-43), God commands the Jewish people: "You shall live in booths seven days; all citizens in Israel shall live in booths, in order that future generations may know that I made the Israelite people live in booths when I brought them out of the land of Egypt..."

Two Interpretations of the "Booths"

  • Actual Physical Huts: The temporary, fragile dwellings the Israelites built for themselves in the harsh desert environment, enduring extreme heat, cold, and the dangers of the wilderness.
  • The "Clouds of Glory" (Ananei HaKavod): The miraculous divine clouds that surrounded and hovered over the Israelite camp in the desert, protecting them from the scorching sun, extreme elements, and hostile enemies. According to this interpretation, the Sukkah symbolizes not human-built shelters but God's own divine protection.
By stepping out of the comfort and security of permanent, sturdy homes to live in a fragile hut with a roof made of branches, the holiday serves as a profound reminder: true security and protection come not from thick walls or material wealth, but from God. The willingness to leave the comforts of home is itself an act of faith.
The Main Mitzvah

The Commandment

The Sukkah

הַסֻּכָּה

The defining mitzvah (commandment) of the holiday is to "dwell" in a Sukkah for seven days. "Dwelling" primarily means eating all significant meals inside the Sukkah, and many people also sleep in it, weather permitting.

Rules of the Sukkah

  • Must have at least two and a half to three walls (which can be made of any material: wood, canvas, fiberglass).
  • The most important part is the roof, called the s'chach. The s'chach must be made of organic material that grew from the ground but has been disconnected from it — like bamboo, pine branches, or palm fronds.
  • The roof must be thick enough to provide more shade than sunlight, but sparse enough that one can see the stars through it at night — symbolizing our reliance on heaven.
  • It is highly customary to beautifully decorate the Sukkah with hanging fruits, paper chains, and artistic decorations.

The Four Species

Arba Minim — The Four Species

אַרְבַּעַת הַמִּינִים

Every day of Sukkot (except Shabbat), it is commanded to take four specific species of plants and wave them together in all six directions — right, left, forward, up, down, and backward — to signify that God's presence fills the entire universe.

אֶתְרוֹג

Etrog

A citrus fruit similar to a large lemon with a distinctive fragrance. It represents those with both Torah knowledge and good deeds.

לוּלָב

Lulav

A closed frond from a date palm tree. It represents those with Torah knowledge (from dates) but fewer good deeds.

הֲדַסִּים

Hadasim

Three myrtle branches, with fragrance but no taste. They represent those with good deeds but little Torah knowledge.

עֲרָבוֹת

Aravot

Two willow branches, with neither taste nor smell. They represent those who lack both Torah knowledge and good deeds — yet are still bound together with the others.

The Deeper Message

A famous Midrash equates the four species to four types of Jewish people. By binding them all together into a single bundle (the Lulav, Hadasim, and Aravot bound together, with the Etrog held in the other hand and brought together for waving), the holiday teaches a profound truth: the Jewish community is only complete and whole when all types of people — the learned and the simple, the righteous and those still growing — are united together. No member of the community is expendable.

Customs & Special Days

Traditions

Ushpizin, Chol HaMoed & Hoshanah Rabbah

אוּשְׁפִּיזִין וּמִנְהָגֵי הַחָג

Ushpizin — Welcoming Mystical Guests

Because Sukkot is a holiday of inclusion and joy, hospitality (hachnasat orchim) is a major theme. Beyond inviting physical guests, there is a beautiful mystical tradition called Ushpizin (Aramaic for "guests"): each night of Sukkot, we symbolically invite one of the seven founding fathers of the Jewish people into the Sukkah.

אַבְרָהָם

הָאִמָּהוֹת

+ The Matriarchs

Many modern traditions also include the matriarchs: Sarah, Miriam, Deborah, Hannah, Abigail, Huldah, and Esther — one for each night alongside their patriarch counterpart. Ushpizin can be recited in Hebrew or adapted into English, and it is a beautiful practice to honor the memory of these great figures at the Sukkah table.

Chol HaMoed — The Intermediate Days

Sukkot lasts for seven days. The first day (or first two days outside of Israel) is a full festival day with restrictions on work, similar to Shabbat. The intermediate days — Chol HaMoed — are semi-festive: people continue to dwell in the Sukkah and wave the Lulav daily, but regular work is permitted.

Hoshanah Rabbah — The Great Salvation

The Final Sealing of Fate

The seventh and final day of Sukkot is called Hoshanah Rabbah (The Great Salvation). It features a special synagogue service where the congregation circles the sanctuary seven times holding the Four Species, followed by the ancient ritual of beating willow branches against the ground.

Tradition holds that while the decree for the year is sealed on Yom Kippur, the final "verdict" is delivered on Hoshanah Rabbah — making it the absolute last opportunity for repentance for the new year. Following Sukkot are the holidays of Shemini Atzeret and Simchat Torah — which are technically a separate but closely related festival.

Sukkot Blessings & Texts

בְּרָכוֹת חַג הַסֻּכּוֹת

1. Candle Lighting Blessing

Hadlakat Nerot — Candle Lighting

Like all major holidays, the festival is brought in with the lighting of candles before sunset. Bracketed words added if it falls on Friday evening (Shabbat).

בָּרוּךְ אַתָּה, יְיָ אֱלֹהֵינוּ, מֶלֶךְ הָעוֹלָם, אֲשֶׁר קִדְּשָׁנוּ בְּמִצְוֹתָיו וְצִוָּנוּ לְהַדְלִיק נֵר שֶׁל [שַׁבָּת וְשֶׁל] יוֹם טוֹב.

Baruch atah, Adonai Eloheinu, Melech haolam, asher kid'shanu b'mitzvotav v'tzivanu l'hadlik ner shel [Shabbat v'shel] Yom Tov.

Blessed are You, Adonai our God, Sovereign of the universe, who has sanctified us with His commandments and commanded us to kindle the light of [Shabbat and of] the Festival.

2. Shehecheyanu — Blessing of Firsts

Shehecheyanu

Recited during candle lighting on the first night, during the first Kiddush, and upon sitting in the Sukkah or shaking the Lulav for the first time that season.

בָּרוּךְ אַתָּה, יְיָ אֱלֹהֵינוּ, מֶלֶךְ הָעוֹלָם, שֶׁהֶחֱיָנוּ וְקִיְּמָנוּ וְהִגִּיעָנוּ לַזְּמַן הַזֶּה.

Baruch atah, Adonai Eloheinu, Melech haolam, shehecheyanu v'kiy'manu v'higiyanu laz'man hazeh.

Blessed are You, Adonai our God, Sovereign of all, who has kept us alive, sustained us, and brought us to this season.

3. Leshev BaSukkah — Blessing for Dwelling in the Sukkah

Leshev BaSukkah

Recited every time one sits down to eat a significant meal (usually involving bread or cake) inside the Sukkah during the holiday.

בָּרוּךְ אַתָּה, יְיָ אֱלֹהֵינוּ, מֶלֶךְ הָעוֹלָם, אֲשֶׁר קִדְּשָׁנוּ בְּמִצְוֹתָיו וְצִוָּנוּ לֵישֵׁב בַּסֻּכָּה.

Baruch atah, Adonai Eloheinu, Melech haolam, asher kid'shanu b'mitzvotav v'tzivanu leishev basukkah.

Blessed are You, Adonai our God, Sovereign of the universe, who has sanctified us with His commandments and commanded us to dwell in the sukkah.

4. Al Netilat Lulav — Blessing for the Four Species

Hold the Lulav, Hadasim, and Aravot in the right hand and the Etrog (held upside-down, tip pointing down) in the left hand. Recite the blessing, then bring the Etrog right-side-up and bring both hands together to shake and wave in all six directions.

Al Netilat Lulav

Recited once a day (except on Shabbat) before the waving of the Four Species. On the first day, the Shehecheyanu is added immediately after.

בָּרוּךְ אַתָּה, יְיָ אֱלֹהֵינוּ, מֶלֶךְ הָעוֹלָם, אֲשֶׁר קִדְּשָׁנוּ בְּמִצְוֹתָיו וְצִוָּנוּ עַל נְטִילַת לוּלָב.

Baruch atah, Adonai Eloheinu, Melech haolam, asher kid'shanu b'mitzvotav v'tzivanu al n'tilat lulav.

Blessed are You, Adonai our God, Sovereign of the universe, who has sanctified us with His commandments and commanded us concerning the taking of the lulav.

Happy Sukkot! May your booth be filled with joy, guests, and the blessings of the harvest.
