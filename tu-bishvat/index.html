About the Holiday

Introduction

What is Tu BiShvat?

מהו טו בשבט

Date: 15th of Shvat (Tu = ט״ו = 15 in Hebrew numerology)

Meaning: The New Year of the Trees — a fiscal new year for agricultural tithes

Also known as: Rosh HaShanah LaIlanot — "New Year for Trees"

Significance: Marks the start of the spring season in the Land of Israel

Tu BiShvat is one of four Jewish "new years" mentioned in the Mishnah (Rosh HaShanah 1:1). In ancient Israel, it marked the cutoff date for calculating which year's agricultural tithes applied to a tree's fruit. Today it is celebrated as a joyful holiday of nature, ecology, and connection to the Land of Israel — often called the Jewish Earth Day.
Origins & History

Background

History of Tu BiShvat

תּוֹלְדוֹת טוּ בִּשְׁבָט

Agricultural Origins

In the Mishnah, the School of Shammai placed the new year for trees on the 1st of Shvat, while the School of Hillel taught it was the 15th. Jewish law follows the School of Hillel, establishing the 15th of Shvat as the annual cutoff for determining which year's ma'asrot (tithes) and orlah (the prohibition on eating fruit from a tree's first three years) applied. By this date in the Land of Israel, the winter rains have mostly subsided and the sap of the trees begins to rise, heralding the coming spring.

Kabbalistic Revival — Tu BiShvat Seder

In the 16th century, the mystics of Safed (Tzfat) in northern Israel, inspired by the Kabbalistic school of Rabbi Isaac Luria (the Ari), developed a special Tu BiShvat seder — modeled loosely after the Passover seder. They elevated the holiday from a dry legal concept into a profound mystical experience of connecting with the divine through the fruits of the earth. The Kabbalists saw eating the fruits of the Land of Israel as an act of cosmic repair (tikkun), elevating holy sparks embedded in the physical world.

Modern Zionist Revival

In the early 20th century, the Zionist pioneers in the Land of Israel embraced Tu BiShvat as a national afforestation holiday. The Jewish National Fund (JNF/KKL) made tree planting a central custom, transforming desolate land into lush forests. Schoolchildren throughout Israel and the Jewish diaspora plant trees on Tu BiShvat to this day. This environmental dimension has made Tu BiShvat a beloved holiday for ecological awareness and Jewish connection to the Land of Israel.

"If you are planting a tree and someone tells you, 'The Messiah has come,' first finish planting the tree, then go out to greet the Messiah."

— Attributed to Rabbi Yochanan ben Zakkai (Avot d'Rabbi Natan 31b)

The Seven Species

Shivat HaMinim

The Seven Species of the Land of Israel

שִׁבְעַת הַמִּינִים

The Torah (Deuteronomy 8:8) praises the Land of Israel with seven special crops. On Tu BiShvat it is customary to eat from all seven species.

Species 1

חִטָּה

Wheat (Chitah)

The most essential grain of the Land of Israel, used for bread and offering — the foundation of life.

Species 2

שְׂעֹרָה

Barley (Se'orah)

The first grain harvested in spring; used in the Omer offering brought to the Temple on Passover.

Species 3

גֶּפֶן

Grapes (Gefen)

Source of wine for Kiddush, Havdalah, and all sacred occasions; a symbol of joy and abundance.

Species 4

תְּאֵנָה

Figs (Te'enah)

One of the most beloved fruits of the ancient Land; figs are eaten fresh or dried and symbolize wisdom and sweetness.

Species 5

רִמּוֹן

Pomegranate (Rimon)

Said to contain 613 seeds, corresponding to the 613 commandments. A symbol of righteousness and beauty.

Species 6

זַיִת

Olives (Zayit)

Source of the purest oil for the Temple Menorah and cooking; a symbol of peace, light, and eternity.

Species 7

תָּמָר

Dates (Tamar)

The sweet fruit of the date palm; the righteous are compared to a palm tree in Psalms (92:13).

The Tu BiShvat Seder

Kabbalistic Seder

The Four Cups & Kabbalistic Worlds

סֵדֶר טוּ בִּשְׁבָט

The Kabbalistic Tu BiShvat seder involves drinking four cups of wine or grape juice — each blending red and white in different ratios — corresponding to the four spiritual worlds of Kabbalah and the four seasons.

First Cup

White Wine

Olam Assiyah — World of Action

Pure white wine represents winter — dormancy, potential, and physical creation. We eat fruits with inedible outer shells (like walnuts, almonds, pomegranates): the external world protecting the inner life.

Second Cup

Mostly White, Dash of Red

Olam Yetzirah — World of Formation

Represents the awakening of spring. We eat fruits with edible skin but inedible pits (olives, dates, avocados): emotion and creativity, where inner experience is still protected.

Third Cup

Mostly Red, Dash of White

Olam Beriyah — World of Creation

Represents full summer bloom. We eat fully edible fruits with no pits (figs, grapes, blueberries): the world of intellect and thought, where wisdom is fully accessible.

Fourth Cup

Deep Red Wine

Olam Atzilut — World of Emanation

Pure red wine represents the divine realm — fire, harvest, and the highest spiritual reality. We contemplate fruits of pure spirit and unity with the divine, beyond physical form.

Customs of the Seder

  • Reading: At each stage, readings from Torah, Talmud, or Kabbalistic texts are shared about trees and the Land of Israel.
  • Blessings: The appropriate fruit blessings are recited over each type of fruit before eating.
  • Intention (Kavanah): Participants are encouraged to meditate on their personal connection to the earth and to renewal.
  • Tree Planting: Many communities organize communal tree planting in Israel or environmental activities in their local communities.
Prayers & Blessings

Liturgy

Tu BiShvat Blessings

בְּרָכוֹת טוּ בִּשְׁבָט

The primary blessings of Tu BiShvat are the fruit blessings recited over the fruits of the seder, and the Shehecheyanu for the occasion.

1. Borei P'ri Ha'etz — Blessing for Tree Fruits

Blessing Over the Fruit of the Tree

Recited before eating any fruit that grows on a tree (including all Seven Species fruits except wheat and barley).

בָּרוּךְ אַתָּה יְיָ אֱלֹהֵינוּ מֶלֶךְ הָעוֹלָם, בּוֹרֵא פְּרִי הָעֵץ.

Baruch atah, Adonai Eloheinu, Melech haolam, borei p'ri ha'etz.

Blessed are You, Adonai our God, Sovereign of all, who creates the fruit of the tree.

2. Borei P'ri Ha'adamah — Blessing for Ground Fruits

Blessing Over the Fruit of the Ground

Recited before eating fruits and vegetables that grow directly from the ground (such as strawberries or wheat-based foods at the seder).

בָּרוּךְ אַתָּה יְיָ אֱלֹהֵינוּ מֶלֶךְ הָעוֹלָם, בּוֹרֵא פְּרִי הָאֲדָמָה.

Baruch atah, Adonai Eloheinu, Melech haolam, borei p'ri ha'adamah.

Blessed are You, Adonai our God, Sovereign of all, who creates the fruit of the earth.

3. Shehecheyanu — Blessing of Firsts

Shehecheyanu

Some have the custom of reciting Shehecheyanu on Tu BiShvat when eating a new fruit for the first time that season.

בָּרוּךְ אַתָּה יְיָ אֱלֹהֵינוּ מֶלֶךְ הָעוֹלָם, שֶׁהֶחֱיָנוּ וְקִיְּמָנוּ וְהִגִּיעָנוּ לַזְּמַן הַזֶּה.

Baruch atah, Adonai Eloheinu, Melech haolam, shehecheyanu v'kiy'manu v'higiyanu laz'man hazeh.

Blessed are You, Adonai our God, Sovereign of all, who has kept us alive, sustained us, and brought us to this season.

Songs & Music

Tu BiShvat Songs

Songs of Tu BiShvat

שִׁירֵי טוּ בִּשְׁבָט

These beloved songs celebrate the almond tree blooming, the land of Israel flowing with milk and honey, and the righteous flourishing like trees.

Hashkediya Porachat — The Almond Tree is Blooming

The most iconic Tu BiShvat song — celebrating the first sign of spring as the almond tree bursts into bloom in the Land of Israel.

הַשְּׁקֵדִיָּה פּוֹרַחַת וְשֶׁמֶשׁ פָּז זוֹרַחַת,
צִפֳּרִים מֵרֹאשׁ כָּל גַּג מְבַשְּׂרוֹת אֶת בּוֹא הֶחָג.

Hashkeidiya porachat v'shemesh paz zorachat,
Tzipporim meirosh kol gag m'vasrot et bo hechag.

The almond tree is blooming and a golden sun is shining,
Birds from every rooftop are announcing the coming of the holiday.

טוּ בִּשְׁבָט הִגִּיעַ, חַג הָאִילָנוֹת,
טוּ בִּשְׁבָט הִגִּיעַ, חַג הָאִילָנוֹת!

Tu BiShvat higia, chag ha'ilanot,
Tu BiShvat higia, chag ha'ilanot!

Tu BiShvat has arrived, the holiday of the trees!
Tu BiShvat has arrived, the holiday of the trees!

Eretz Zavat Chalav — A Land Flowing with Milk and Honey

A beautiful song celebrating the abundance of the Land of Israel and its seven species.

אֶרֶץ זָבַת חָלָב וּדְבַשׁ,
אֶרֶץ חִטָּה וּשְׂעֹרָה,
גֶּפֶן וּתְאֵנָה וְרִמּוֹן,
אֶרֶץ זֵית שֶׁמֶן וּדְבַשׁ.

Eretz zavat chalav udvash,
Eretz chitah us'orah,
Gefen ut'einah v'rimon,
Eretz zayit shemen udvash.

A land flowing with milk and honey,
A land of wheat and barley,
Grapes and figs and pomegranates,
A land of olive oil and honey.

Tzadik Katamar Yifrach — The Righteous Shall Flourish

From Psalm 92 — a Shabbat psalm that beautifully connects the righteous person to the flourishing of trees. Often sung at the Tu BiShvat seder.

צַדִּיק כַּתָּמָר יִפְרָח,
כְּאֶרֶז בַּלְּבָנוֹן יִשְׂגֶּה.
שְׁתוּלִים בְּבֵית יְיָ,
בְּחַצְרוֹת אֱלֹהֵינוּ יַפְרִיחוּ.

Tzadik katamar yifrach,
K'erez baLevanon yisgeh.
Sh'tulim b'veit Adonai,
B'chatzrot Eloheinu yafrichu.

The righteous shall flourish like the date palm,
They will grow like a cedar in Lebanon.
Planted in the house of God,
They will flourish in the courts of our God.

עוֹד יְנוּבוּן בְּשֵׂיבָה,
דְּשֵׁנִים וְרַעֲנַנִּים יִהְיוּ.

Od y'nuvun b'seivah,
D'shenim v'ra'ananim yih'yu.

They shall still produce fruit in old age,
Vigorous and fresh they will remain.

Ki Tavo'u El Ha'aretz — When You Come to the Land

A song based on the biblical commandment about orlah (Leviticus 19:23) — the first-fruits law that connects Tu BiShvat to its agricultural roots.

כִּי תָבֹאוּ אֶל הָאָרֶץ,
וּנְטַעְתֶּם כָּל עֵץ מַאֲכָל,
וַעֲרַלְתֶּם עָרְלָתוֹ אֶת פִּרְיוֹ,
שָׁלוֹשׁ שָׁנִים יִהְיֶה לָכֶם עֲרֵלִים.

Ki tavo'u el ha'aretz,
Un'tatem kol eitz ma'achal,
Va'araltem orlato et piryo,
Shalosh shanim yihyeh lachem arelim.

When you come to the land
And plant all kinds of food trees,
You shall regard its fruit as forbidden —
For three years it shall be as if uncircumcised.

!חַג טוּ בִּשְׁבָט שָׂמֵחַ

Happy Tu BiShvat! May you be blessed with deep roots and flourishing branches.
