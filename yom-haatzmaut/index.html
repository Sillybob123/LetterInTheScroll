Overview

What Is Yom Ha'atzmaut?

Yom Ha'atzmaut is the national independence day of Israel, commemorating the formal declaration of the State of Israel by David Ben-Gurion in Tel Aviv on May 14, 1948 — which corresponded to the 5th of Iyar, 5708.

Unlike the ancient, biblically mandated festivals, Yom Ha'atzmaut is a modern addition to the Jewish calendar. However, for the vast majority of world Jewry, it holds immense spiritual and religious significance. After 2,000 years of exile, the establishment of a sovereign Jewish state in the biblical Land of Israel is viewed as an open miracle and a watershed moment in Jewish history.

The holiday is immediately preceded by Yom HaZikaron — Israel's Memorial Day for fallen soldiers and victims of terrorism — making the transition to independence day one of the most emotionally profound experiences in the Israeli calendar.

History

The Historical Origins

The Long Exile and the Zionist Movement

Following the destruction of the Second Temple in 70 CE by the Romans, the Jewish people were scattered across the globe in what became known as the Diaspora. Despite centuries of exile, the daily liturgy and culture remained fiercely tied to the dream of returning to Zion. The thrice-daily prayers for the rebuilding of Jerusalem never faded.

In the late 19th century, this ancient spiritual longing transformed into a modern political movement called Zionism, spearheaded by figures like Theodor Herzl, which sought to establish a legally recognized Jewish home in the Land of Israel (then Palestine under Ottoman and later British rule).

The UN Partition and the Declaration

On November 29, 1947, the United Nations adopted Resolution 181, proposing to partition the British Mandate of Palestine into independent Arab and Jewish states. While Jewish leaders accepted the plan, Arab leaders rejected it, leading to the outbreak of the 1947–1949 war.

On May 14, 1948, hours before the British Mandate was set to expire at midnight, David Ben-Gurion gathered Jewish leaders at the Tel Aviv Museum of Art and read aloud the Israeli Declaration of Independence, establishing the State of Israel. The new state immediately opened its doors to Jewish refugees worldwide. Within minutes, the United States and the Soviet Union extended diplomatic recognition.

Customs & Traditions

How Yom Ha'atzmaut Is Celebrated

  • 1
    The Transition from Yom HaZikaron

    Perhaps the most unique and emotionally jarring aspect of Yom Ha'atzmaut is how it begins. It is immediately preceded by Yom HaZikaron (Israel's Memorial Day for Fallen Soldiers). For 24 hours, the country is in deep, collective mourning. At exactly 8:00 PM, a dramatic shift occurs: the national flag is raised from half-mast to the top of the pole, and Israel erupts in joy. The sudden transition from agonizing grief to ecstatic celebration reflects a profound Israeli ethos — the joy of independence is only possible because of the ultimate sacrifices made the day before.

  • 2
    The Torch-Lighting Ceremony (Tekes Hadlakat Masuot)

    The official start of Yom Ha'atzmaut is marked by a massive, nationally televised ceremony on Mount Herzl in Jerusalem. Twelve torches are lit by distinguished citizens chosen for outstanding contributions to Israeli society, representing the Twelve Tribes of Israel. Each torch lighting is concluded with the proud declaration: "Le'tiferet Medinat Yisrael!" — "To the glory of the State of Israel!"

  • 3
    The Mangal (Israeli BBQ)

    The most widespread secular custom on Yom Ha'atzmaut is the mangal — a traditional Israeli barbecue. Millions of Israelis spend the day in national parks, beaches, and backyards, grilling meats, socializing, and enjoying the outdoors. The mangal has become the quintessential symbol of Israeli independence day celebrations.

  • 4
    The Air Force Flyover (Mataz)

    During the day, the Israeli Air Force performs a nationwide flyover known as Mataz. Fighter jets, helicopters, and transport planes fly across the entire country from the northern borders to the southern tip in Eilat, as citizens gather on rooftops and beaches to watch the aerial acrobatics — a breathtaking display of the miraculous strength of the modern Jewish state.

Blessings & Prayers

Special Prayers of Thanksgiving

Many synagogues incorporate special prayers of thanksgiving on Yom Ha'atzmaut to acknowledge the miraculous nature of Israel's founding. In Religious Zionist congregations, the full Hallel (Psalms 113–118) is recited aloud — just as on Passover and Sukkot — formally thanking G-d for the miracle of the State.

Prayer for the State of Israel — Avinu Shebashamayim
אָבִינוּ שֶׁבַּשָּׁמַיִם, צוּר יִשְׂרָאֵל וְגוֹאֲלוֹ, בָּרֵךְ אֶת מְדִינַת יִשְׂרָאֵל, רֵאשִׁית צְמִיחַת גְּאֻלָּתֵנוּ.
Avinu shebashamayim, Tzur Yisrael v'go'alo, bareich et Medinat Yisrael, reishit tz'michat g'ulateinu.
Our Father in Heaven, Rock and Redeemer of Israel, bless the State of Israel, the first flourishing of our redemption. Shield it with Your lovingkindness, envelop it in Your peace, and bestow Your light and truth upon its leaders.

This majestic prayer was authored by the Chief Rabbinate of Israel shortly after the state was established. It asks for divine protection over the land, its leaders, and its defenders — and is recited in synagogues around the world on Yom Ha'atzmaut and every Shabbat.

Frequently Asked

Yom Ha'atzmaut FAQ

"Yom Ha'atzmaut" (יוֹם הָעַצְמָאוּת) translates to "Day of Independence" in Hebrew. It commemorates Israel's declaration of independence on the 5th of Iyar, 5708 (May 14, 1948).
Yom Ha'atzmaut is primarily a national holiday, but for much of the Jewish world it carries deep religious significance. Religious Zionist communities recite the full Hallel (Psalms of Praise) and special prayers, viewing the day as a miraculous fulfillment of the biblical promise of the return to Zion. Some ultra-Orthodox communities observe the day with fewer or no religious ceremonies.
Yom HaZikaron (Israel's Memorial Day for Fallen Soldiers and Victims of Terrorism) immediately precedes Yom Ha'atzmaut. The deliberate juxtaposition carries a profound message: the independence and joy of the Jewish state were purchased at great cost. The transition — from a siren-filled day of mourning to a night of fireworks and celebration — is one of the most striking cultural moments in Israeli life.
Hatikvah ("The Hope") is the national anthem of Israel. Originally written as a poem in 1878 by Naftali Herz Imber, it expresses the 2,000-year-old yearning of the Jewish people to return to their ancestral homeland and live as a free nation in the Land of Israel. It is the most powerful symbol of Zionist aspiration and is sung at virtually every official ceremony on Yom Ha'atzmaut.
