About the Day

Introduction

What is Yom HaZikaron?

מהו יום הזיכרון

Yom HaZikaron (Hebrew: יוֹם הַזִּכָּרוֹן), formally known as Yom HaZikaron LeHalalei Ma'arakhot Yisrael ul'Nifge'ei Peulot HaEivah — Memorial Day for the Fallen Soldiers of the Wars of Israel and Victims of Actions of Terrorism — is the national day of mourning in Israel.

Unlike Memorial Day in many other countries, which can sometimes feel like a long weekend, Yom HaZikaron is a day of profound, active mourning. It honors:

Fallen Soldiers: All members of the IDF, police, Mossad, Shin Bet, and pre-state underground organizations (like the Haganah or Irgun) who died in the line of duty.

Victims of Terror: Civilians who have been killed in acts of terrorism against Israel and the Jewish people.

Significance

Why Is It So Important?

מדוע יום זה כה משמעותי

To understand Yom HaZikaron is to understand the Israeli psyche. Because of mandatory military service, almost every Israeli family is a "bereaved family" or is closely connected to one.

National Survival

It reinforces the idea that the State of Israel does not exist by chance, but through the sacrifice of its citizens — generation after generation.

Unity

It is one of the few days that almost completely bridges the gap between religious, secular, left-wing, and right-wing Israelis. The shared grief creates a powerful national bond.

The Price of Freedom

It serves as a constant reminder that Jewish sovereignty comes with a heavy, ongoing cost — one that must never be forgotten or taken for granted.

How the Day Is Observed

Rituals

Key Traditions and Rituals

מנהגי יום הזיכרון

The Sirens — A Nation Stops

The most iconic and moving part of the day occurs twice. During these sirens, the entire country stops. Cars pull over on highways and drivers stand beside them; pedestrians stop walking; shops pause transactions. There is a total, haunting silence across the land.

8:00 PM

One-minute siren at the start of the evening, marking the beginning of Yom HaZikaron. The entire country falls silent.

11:00 AM

Two-minute siren the following morning — the longest silence. Cities, highways, and workplaces all come to a complete halt.

Closures and National Atmosphere

By law, all places of entertainment are closed — cinemas, restaurants, pubs, theaters. Radio and TV stations switch their programming entirely to documentaries about the fallen, memorial ceremonies, and somber music.

Dam HaMaccabim — Blood of the Maccabees

The Red Anemone

The symbol of the day is a small red flower called Dam HaMaccabim (Blood of the Maccabees). Legend says that where this flower grows, a drop of a hero's blood once fell. People wear a sticker of this flower on their shirts throughout the day as a mark of solidarity and remembrance.

Memorial Ceremonies

  • The Western Wall: The official opening ceremony takes place here on the eve of the day, with the national torch lighting.
  • Mount Herzl: The main national ceremony occurs at the military cemetery on Mount Herzl in Jerusalem, attended by the President, Prime Minister, and Chief of Staff.
  • Schools and Communities: Every school and community center holds its own Tekes (ceremony) where the names of local fallen are read and candles are lit.

The date is officially the 4th of Iyar on the Hebrew calendar. It is sometimes shifted by a day to avoid coinciding with the Sabbath — when the sirens and ceremonies would be prohibited.

Note for the Diaspora: While the observance is primarily an Israeli state occasion, many Jewish communities worldwide hold parallel memorial ceremonies on this day, standing in solidarity with Israel's bereaved families and acknowledging the shared Jewish connection to those who fell defending the Jewish homeland.

The Turning Point

From Mourning to Joy

מאבל לשמחה

One of the most unique and emotionally powerful aspects of Israeli culture is the transition from Yom HaZikaron directly into Yom Ha'atzmaut (Independence Day).

At nightfall on the day of Memorial, a ceremony at Mount Herzl marks the end of mourning and the start of celebration. The flag is raised from half-mast to the top of the pole. The "switch" is jarring and difficult for many — moving from deep grief to ecstatic celebration in a matter of minutes — but it is intentional.

The Profound Message

The seamless transition from Yom HaZikaron to Yom Ha'atzmaut symbolizes that the independence being celebrated was bought with the lives of those being mourned. You cannot understand the joy of the one without feeling the weight of the other. The State of Israel was born through sacrifice — and it survives through sacrifice.

Recent History

Contemporary Context

The Impact of October 7

השפעת ה-7 באוקטובר

In recent years, the weight of Yom HaZikaron has grown exponentially for the entire Jewish world.

The events of October 7, 2023, and the subsequent war in Gaza, have added thousands of names to the list of the fallen and victims of terror. For many Israelis, the "old" grief of past wars has been layered with the very "raw" and "fresh" grief of the present, making the annual observance particularly intense and painful.

A Different Kind of Memorial

Yom HaZikaron 2024 and 2025 were unlike any before them. Families of hostages still held in Gaza attended ceremonies alongside families of soldiers who had fallen in combat, creating a new and heartbreaking dimension to the day. The hostages are not yet counted among the fallen — yet the uncertainty of their fate adds a layer of ongoing anguish that has no historical parallel in modern Israeli memory.

For Jews everywhere, this makes Yom HaZikaron 2026 a particularly solemn and significant occasion — a day to stand together in solidarity with Israel, to say the names of the fallen, and to pray for the swift return of those still held captive.
