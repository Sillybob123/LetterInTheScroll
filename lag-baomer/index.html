About the Holiday

Introduction

What is Lag BaOmer?

מהו ל״ג בעומר

Date: 18 Iyar — the 33rd day of the Omer count (between Passover and Shavuot)

Name: ל״ג = 33 in Hebrew numerals; בָּעֹמֶר = "in the Omer"

Status: A minor holiday; a joyful interruption in the semi-mourning Omer period

Main customs: Bonfires, haircuts (Upsherin), bows and arrows, pilgrimages

The period of the Omer — the 49 days between Passover and Shavuot — is traditionally observed as a time of semi-mourning: no weddings, no haircuts, and no live music. Lag BaOmer breaks this solemnity, offering a day of celebration, joy, and outdoor festivities. Hundreds of thousands of Jews make a pilgrimage to the tomb of Rabbi Shimon bar Yochai in Meron, in northern Israel, on this day.
The Two Reasons

Historical Background

Why is Lag BaOmer Celebrated?

טַעֲמֵי הַחַג

Two distinct historical events are associated with the joyfulness of Lag BaOmer.

Reason 1 — The Plague of Rabbi Akiva's Students Ends

The Talmud (Yevamot 62b) records that Rabbi Akiva — the great 2nd-century sage — had 24,000 students, and they all died in a terrible plague between Passover and Shavuot. The reason given is that they did not treat each other with sufficient respect. This tragedy is why the Omer period is observed as a time of mourning. According to tradition, the plague ceased on Lag BaOmer — the 33rd day of the Omer — making it a day of relief and joy.

Rabbi Akiva and the Bar Kokhba Revolt

Many historians connect the death of Rabbi Akiva's students to the Bar Kokhba revolt against Roman rule (132–135 CE). Rabbi Akiva famously supported Bar Kokhba as a potential Messiah. The "24,000 students" may be a euphemism for the Jewish soldiers and scholars who died in the failed revolt. On Lag BaOmer, a small victory was celebrated. After the revolt's ultimate failure, Rabbi Akiva was martyred by the Romans — his death is commemorated on Yom Kippur.

Reason 2 — The Passing of Rabbi Shimon bar Yochai

The most spiritually significant reason for Lag BaOmer is that it is the yahrzeit (death anniversary) of Rabbi Shimon bar Yochai (Rashbi), the great Tanna and mystic, and one of Rabbi Akiva's greatest surviving students. Rabbi Shimon bar Yochai is traditionally credited with authoring the Zohar, the foundational text of Jewish Kabbalah. He died on Lag BaOmer, and according to the Zohar, before his death he revealed the deepest secrets of the Torah in a blinding burst of divine light. He instructed his students that his death should not be mourned but celebrated as a day of joy and spiritual revelation.

"This day should be for you a day of rejoicing. I myself will celebrate with you this day."

— Rabbi Shimon bar Yochai on the day of his passing (Idra Zuta, Zohar)

The Cave and the Carob Tree

Rabbi Shimon bar Yochai and his son Elazar were forced to hide in a cave from the Romans for 13 years. During this time, a miraculous carob tree and spring of water sustained them. Hidden in the cave, Rabbi Shimon reached the highest levels of Torah knowledge and mystical insight. His survival in the cave is symbolized by the bonfires of Lag BaOmer — the light of the Torah that sustains even in darkness.

Traditions

Customs & Traditions

מִנְהָגֵי ל״ג בָּעֹמֶר

1. Bonfires (Meduroth)

The lighting of bonfires is the most iconic Lag BaOmer custom. Fires symbolize the great light of the Torah that Rabbi Shimon bar Yochai revealed at his death. In Israel, children and adults build bonfires in parks, fields, and backyards across the country, cooking, singing, and dancing through the night. The largest bonfire of all is lit by the Boyaner Rebbe at the tomb of Rabbi Shimon bar Yochai in Meron, marking the beginning of the celebrations.

2. Pilgrimage to Meron

Each year, hundreds of thousands of Jews make a pilgrimage to the cave-tomb of Rabbi Shimon bar Yochai in Meron, in the Galilee region of northern Israel. They pray, sing, dance, and light memorial candles at his tomb all night and into the next day. It is the largest annual gathering in Israel and one of the largest pilgrimages in the Jewish world.

3. Upsherin — First Haircuts

Many Jewish families give their three-year-old boys their first haircut (called Upsherin in Yiddish or Chalakeh in Hebrew) on Lag BaOmer, often at the tomb of Rabbi Shimon bar Yochai in Meron. Until this age, the boy's hair is left to grow freely; the first haircut symbolizes the beginning of formal Torah education. The age of three is connected to the concept of orlah — just as a tree's first three years' fruit is forbidden, a child's first three years are a time of pure nurturing before the journey of Jewish education begins.

4. Bows and Arrows

Children play with toy bows and arrows on Lag BaOmer — a custom with several explanations. Some say it commemorates the military tactics of Bar Kokhba's fighters. Others note that the Hebrew word for rainbow (keshet, קֶשֶׁת) was also used for a bow-and-arrow, and that during Rabbi Shimon bar Yochai's lifetime, a rainbow was never seen in the sky — a sign that his merit alone was sufficient to protect the world from a second flood (rainbows being the sign of the first covenant). When Rashbi was present, no rainbow was needed.

The Omer Counting

Sefirat HaOmer

Counting the Omer — Day 33

סְפִירַת הָעֹמֶר

Each night from the second night of Passover through the eve of Shavuot, we count the Omer — the 49 days connecting the physical freedom of Passover to the spiritual freedom of receiving the Torah. On Lag BaOmer, we count the 33rd day.

33

יוֹם שְׁלֹשָׁה וּשְׁלֹשִׁים לָעֹמֶר

which is four weeks and five days of the Omer

Blessing for Counting the Omer

Sefirat HaOmer — Omer Counting Blessing

Recited each night after nightfall. The blessing is followed by the count for that specific day.

בָּרוּךְ אַתָּה יְיָ אֱלֹהֵינוּ מֶלֶךְ הָעוֹלָם, אֲשֶׁר קִדְּשָׁנוּ בְּמִצְוֹתָיו וְצִוָּנוּ עַל סְפִירַת הָעֹמֶר.

Baruch atah, Adonai Eloheinu, Melech haolam, asher kid'shanu b'mitzvotav v'tzivanu al sefirat ha'omer.

Blessed are You, Adonai our God, Sovereign of all, who has sanctified us with Your commandments and commanded us concerning the counting of the Omer.

The Count for Lag BaOmer — Day 33

Today is Thirty-Three Days of the Omer

Recited on the night of Lag BaOmer, after the above blessing.

הַיּוֹם שְׁלֹשָׁה וּשְׁלֹשִׁים יוֹם, שֶׁהֵם אַרְבָּעָה שָׁבוּעוֹת וַחֲמִשָּׁה יָמִים לָעֹמֶר.

Hayom shloshah ushloashim yom, shehem arba'ah shavuot vachamishah yamim la'omer.

Today is thirty-three days, which are four weeks and five days of the Omer.

The Kabbalistic Meaning of Day 33

Each day of the Omer corresponds to a combination of the seven lower sefirot (divine attributes) of Kabbalah: Chesed (loving-kindness), Gevurah (strength), Tiferet (beauty), Netzach (eternity), Hod (splendor), Yesod (foundation), and Malchut (sovereignty). Day 33 corresponds to Hod shebe'Hod — Splendor within Splendor. This is deeply connected to Rabbi Shimon bar Yochai, who is associated with Hod, and whose passing radiated transcendent divine light.

Lag BaOmer Songs

Songs of Lag BaOmer

שִׁירֵי ל״ג בָּעֹמֶר

These beloved songs are sung around the bonfires and in celebrations throughout Lag BaOmer — honoring Rabbi Akiva, Rabbi Shimon bar Yochai, and the light of the Torah.

Amar Rabbi Akiva — Rabbi Akiva Said

Based on Rabbi Akiva's famous teaching that "Love your neighbor as yourself" is the greatest principle in the Torah — sung as a round and a celebration of the bond between all Jews.

אָמַר רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא:
אָהַבְתָּ לְרֵעֲךָ כָּמוֹךָ —
זֶה כְּלָל גָּדוֹל בַּתּוֹרָה,
זֶה כְּלָל גָּדוֹל בַּתּוֹרָה!

Amar Rabbi Akiva:
V'ahavta l'rei'acha kamocha —
Zeh k'lal gadol baTorah,
Zeh k'lal gadol baTorah!

Rabbi Akiva said:
"Love your neighbor as yourself" —
This is the great principle of the Torah,
This is the great principle of the Torah!

Ve'Amartem Koh LeChai — And You Shall Say: To Life!

A joyful, upbeat song of blessing sung at celebrations and around the bonfire on Lag BaOmer — based on the verse from Samuel 1 (25:6).

וַאֲמַרְתֶּם כֹּה לֶחָי!
וְאַתָּה שָׁלוֹם, וּבֵיתְךָ שָׁלוֹם,
וְכֹל אֲשֶׁר לְךָ שָׁלוֹם!

Ve'amartem koh lechai!
V'atah shalom, uveotcha shalom,
V'chol asher l'cha shalom!

And you shall say: To life!
And you shall be well, and your household shall be well,
And all that you have shall be well!

Bar Yochai — Son of Yochai

A majestic Kabbalistic poem by Rabbi Shimon ibn Lavi (16th century), sung to honor Rabbi Shimon bar Yochai. The refrain is sung at Meron and at Shabbat tables throughout the Sephardic world.
