About the Holiday

Introduction

What is Shavuot Exactly?

מהו חג השבועות

Date: 6th of Sivan — 50 days after Passover

Meaning: "Weeks" (referring to the seven weeks counted from Passover)

Liturgical Name: Z'man Matan Torateinu — The Time of the Giving of Our Torah

Type: One of the Shalosh Regalim (Three Pilgrimage Festivals)

Shavuot holds dual significance: historically, it commemorates the momentous occasion of the giving of the Torah by God to the Jewish people at Mount Sinai. Agriculturally, it originated as a harvest festival marking the end of the spring barley harvest and the beginning of the summer wheat harvest in the Land of Israel. In ancient times, Israelite males were commanded to appear before God in Jerusalem, bringing offerings of the first fruits (Bikkurim) to the Holy Temple.

Physical vs. Spiritual Freedom

While Passover represents the physical freedom from Egyptian bondage, Shavuot represents the spiritual freedom achieved through the acceptance of the Torah's laws and ethics. As Jewish teachings often state, true freedom is found within the boundaries of moral law. The Exodus was incomplete without the Torah — the destination, not just the departure.

The Meaning of the Holiday

Nomenclature

The Four Names of the Holiday

ארבעת שמות החג

Shavuot is known by four distinct names in Jewish tradition, each highlighting a different dimension of its meaning.

Name 1

חַג הַשָּׁבֻעוֹת

Chag HaShavuot — Feast of Weeks

The Torah commands the counting of seven full weeks from the second day of Passover. Shavuot literally means "weeks," symbolizing the completion of this 49-day period — the Counting of the Omer.

Name 2

זְמַן מַתַּן תּוֹרָתֵנוּ

Z'man Matan Torateinu

Meaning "The Time of the Giving of Our Torah." This is the liturgical name used in holiday prayers, reflecting the rabbinic calculation that the Torah was given on the 6th of Sivan at Mount Sinai.

Name 3

חַג הַבִּכּוּרִים

Chag HaBikkurim — Festival of First Fruits

In Temple times, this holiday marked the beginning of the season when farmers would bring the first ripe fruits of the "Seven Species" of the Land of Israel to Jerusalem as an offering.

Name 4

חַג הַקָּצִיר

Chag HaKatzir — Festival of Reaping

Connected to its agricultural roots, this name highlights Shavuot as the celebration of the wheat harvest in ancient Israel, when the entire nation would come to Jerusalem to offer thanks.

The Story

Historical Event

The Giving of the Torah at Sinai

מַתַּן תּוֹרָה בְּהַר סִינַי

According to the Book of Exodus (chapters 19 and 20), the Israelites arrived at the foot of Mount Sinai in the third month after fleeing slavery in Egypt.

They camped opposite the mountain, and God instructed Moses to prepare the people for three days. On the morning of the third day, there was thunder, lightning, a thick cloud upon the mountain, and the incredibly loud blast of a shofar (ram's horn). Mount Sinai was wrapped in smoke because God descended upon it in fire, and the whole mountain trembled violently.

From this awe-inspiring display, God spoke the Ten Commandments to the entire assembly of Israel — not through an intermediary, but directly to the people. This was a unique moment of national prophecy in human history.

"Na'aseh v'nishma" — "We will do and we will hear/understand"

— The Israelites' famous declaration of total commitment before receiving the Torah

All Souls Were Present

Rabbinic tradition teaches that the revelation at Sinai was unique — the souls of all Jews, past, present, and future, were present to hear the divine voice and enter into the covenant. Every Jewish soul stood at Sinai. This is why Shavuot feels deeply personal: it is not just a historical memory but a moment each person re-experiences.

Special Reading

The Story of Ruth

מְגִלַּת רוּת

In synagogues on Shavuot, it is a universal custom to read the Book of Ruth (Megillat Ruth) — a profound story of loyalty, loving-kindness, and faith.

Set during the time of the Judges, a famine in Israel forces a Jewish family (Elimelech, his wife Naomi, and their two sons) to relocate to the neighboring land of Moab. The sons marry Moabite women — Ruth and Orpah. Tragically, all three men die, leaving Naomi and her daughters-in-law as childless widows.

Hearing that the famine in Israel has ended, Naomi decides to return to Bethlehem and urges her daughters-in-law to remain in Moab. Orpah reluctantly stays, but Ruth clings to Naomi, declaring one of the most famous professions of faith in all of Scripture:

"Do not urge me to leave you, to turn back and not follow you. For wherever you go, I will go; wherever you lodge, I will lodge; your people shall be my people, and your God my God."

— Ruth 1:16

Arriving in Bethlehem at the beginning of the barley harvest, Ruth goes to glean grain in the fields to support Naomi — exercising the Torah law that commands farmers to leave the corners of their fields for the poor. She happens upon the field of Boaz, a wealthy relative of Naomi, who notices her modesty and kindness and protects her. Eventually, Boaz fulfills the role of the "redeemer," marrying Ruth. Their son Obed becomes the grandfather of King David.

Why We Read Ruth on Shavuot

  • The Harvest Setting: The story takes place entirely during the barley and wheat harvests, mirroring Shavuot's identity as the Harvest Festival.
  • Conversion and Acceptance of Torah: Ruth's willing acceptance of Judaism parallels the Israelites' acceptance of the Torah at Sinai. She is considered the archetype of all righteous converts.
  • King David's Lineage: The genealogy at the end of Ruth traces Obed → Jesse → King David. According to tradition, King David was born and died on Shavuot.
  • The Theme of Chesed: The rabbis note that Ruth was written to teach the great reward for acts of loving-kindness — the very foundation of the Torah's ethical teaching.

Traditions

Customs & Traditions

מִנְהָגֵי הַחָג

Eating Dairy Foods

It is a widespread custom to eat dairy meals on Shavuot, with cheesecake and blintzes being quintessential favorites. Several reasons are offered in Jewish literature:

  • Kosher Laws: Before receiving the Torah, Israelites weren't obligated to follow dietary laws (kashrut). Upon receiving the Torah at Sinai, their meat pots were no longer kosher, so they opted for dairy until they could properly prepare kosher meat.
  • Milk and Honey: The Torah is compared to milk and honey in the Song of Songs (4:11): "Honey and milk are under your tongue."
  • Gematria: The numerical value of the Hebrew word for milk (חָלָב, chalav) is 40, corresponding to the 40 days Moses spent on Mount Sinai receiving the Torah.

Tikkun Leil Shavuot — All-Night Torah Study

Many Jews observe the custom of staying awake the entire first night of Shavuot studying Torah. A Midrash states that the night before the Torah was given, the Israelites fell asleep and Moses had to wake them. To "rectify" this — and to demonstrate the love and eagerness that the Israelites lacked — Jews demonstrate their dedication by staying up all night studying.

Decorating with Flowers

Homes and synagogues are traditionally decorated with flowers, branches, and greenery. A Midrash tells that Mount Sinai, normally a barren desert mountain, burst into bloom with fragrant flowers and lush greenery in honor of the giving of the Torah.

Special Liturgy — Akdamut

Synagogue services include the recitation of Hallel (Psalms of Praise) and the Yizkor memorial service on the final day. In Ashkenazi communities, a famous medieval Aramaic poem called Akdamut is chanted before the Torah reading on the first day, extolling God's greatness and the Jewish people's dedication to the Torah.

Prayers & Blessings

Liturgy

Shavuot Blessings & Texts

בְּרָכוֹת וּתְפִלּוֹת

Like all major Jewish holidays, Shavuot begins with candle lighting, followed by the festival Kiddush over a cup of wine or grape juice, and the Shehecheyanu blessing.

1. Shehecheyanu — Blessing of Firsts

Shehecheyanu

Recited upon lighting the holiday candles and at the end of the Kiddush, thanking God for sustaining us to reach this joyful season.

בָּרוּךְ אַתָּה, יְיָ אֱלֹהֵינוּ, מֶלֶךְ הָעוֹלָם, שֶׁהֶחֱיָנוּ וְקִיְּמָנוּ וְהִגִּיעָנוּ לַזְּמַן הַזֶּה.

Baruch atah, Adonai Eloheinu, Melech haolam, shehecheyanu v'kiy'manu v'higiyanu laz'man hazeh.

Blessed are You, Adonai our God, Sovereign of all, who has kept us alive, sustained us, and brought us to this season.

2. Festival Kiddush — Evening Blessing

The Kiddush sanctifies the holiday. The leader recites over a full cup of wine or grape juice. If Shavuot falls on a Friday night, special Shabbat insertions are added (bracketed).

Kiddush for Shavuot

בָּרוּךְ אַתָּה יְיָ אֱלֹהֵינוּ מֶלֶךְ הָעוֹלָם, בּוֹרֵא פְּרִי הַגָּפֶן. בָּרוּךְ אַתָּה יְיָ אֱלֹהֵינוּ מֶלֶךְ הָעוֹלָם, אֲשֶׁר בָּחַר בָּנוּ מִכָּל עָם וְרוֹמְמָנוּ מִכָּל לָשׁוֹן, וְקִדְּשָׁנוּ בְּמִצְוֹתָיו. וַתִּתֶּן לָנוּ יְיָ אֱלֹהֵינוּ בְּאַהֲבָה מוֹעֲדִים לְשִׂמְחָה, חַגִּים וּזְמַנִּים לְשָׂשׂוֹן, אֶת יוֹם חַג הַשָּׁבֻעוֹת הַזֶּה, זְמַן מַתַּן תּוֹרָתֵנוּ מִקְרָא קֹדֶשׁ, זֵכֶר לִיצִיאַת מִצְרָיִם. כִּי בָנוּ בָחַרְתָּ וְאוֹתָנוּ קִדַּשְׁתָּ מִכָּל הָעַמִּים, וּמוֹעֲדֵי קָדְשְׁךָ בְּשִׂמְחָה וּבְשָׂשׂוֹן הִנְחַלְתָּנוּ. בָּרוּךְ אַתָּה יְיָ, מְקַדֵּשׁ יִשְׂרָאֵל וְהַזְּמַנִּים.

Baruch atah, Adonai Eloheinu, Melech haolam, borei p'ri hagafen. Baruch atah, Adonai Eloheinu, Melech haolam, asher bachar banu mikol am, v'rom'manu mikol lashon, v'kid'shanu b'mitzvotav. Vatiten lanu, Adonai Eloheinu, b'ahavah mo-adim l'simchah, chagim uz'manim l'sason, et yom Chag HaShavuot hazeh, z'man matan Torateinu, mikra kodesh, zeicher litziat Mitzrayim. Ki vanu vacharta v'otanu kidashta mikol haamim umo-adei kodsh'cha b'simchah uv'sason hinchaltanu. Baruch atah, Adonai m'kadeish Yisrael v'hazmanim.

Praise to You, Adonai our God, Sovereign of the universe, who creates the fruit of the vine. Praise to You, Adonai our God, Sovereign of the universe, who has chosen us from among the peoples, exalting us by hallowing us with mitzvot. In Your love, You have given us feasts of gladness and seasons of joy: this Festival of Shavuot, season of our receiving Torah, a sacred occasion, a remembrance of the Exodus from Egypt. For You have chosen and consecrated us, and given us the Festivals of joy. Praised be You, Adonai, who sanctifies Israel and the Festivals.
