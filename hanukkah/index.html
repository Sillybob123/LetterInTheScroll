About the Holiday

Introduction

What is Hanukkah?

מהי חנוכה

Date: 25 Kislev — lasts eight days

Meaning: "Dedication" — referring to the rededication of the Holy Temple

Also Known As: Chag HaUrim — the Festival of Lights

Type: Rabbinic holiday (not Biblical); work is permitted

Hanukkah commemorates two interwoven miracles: the military victory of a small, faithful band of Jewish fighters against the vastly more powerful Greek-Syrian army, and the miraculous burning of a single cruse of pure olive oil for eight days in the rededicated Temple. It is observed by lighting the Hanukkiah (an eight-branched menorah), reciting blessings, singing songs, playing dreidel, and eating oil-fried foods.
The Story

Historical Events

The Maccabean Revolt & the Miracle of the Oil

מַעֲשֵׂה חֲנֻכָּה

Greek Oppression — Antiochus IV

In 167 BCE, the Greek-Syrian king Antiochus IV Epiphanes ("God Manifest") launched a fierce campaign to erase Jewish identity and assimilate the Jews into Hellenistic culture. He outlawed the observance of Shabbat, Torah study, circumcision, and the Jewish calendar. He desecrated the Holy Temple in Jerusalem — installing an altar to Zeus in the Temple courtyard and sacrificing pigs on the altar. Those who refused to submit faced execution.

The Maccabean Revolt

In the town of Modi'in, an elderly priest named Mattathias of the Hasmonean family refused the royal decree. When a Hellenized Jew stepped forward to offer a pagan sacrifice, Mattathias killed him and the king's officer. He and his five sons fled to the Judean hills, where they launched a guerrilla campaign. After Mattathias's death, his son Judah — nicknamed "HaMaccabee" (the Hammer) — took command. Against all military odds, the Maccabees defeated the powerful Greek-Syrian army through faith, strategy, and fierce devotion to God.

"Mi LaHashem Elai!" — "Whoever is for God, come to me!"

— Mattathias's rallying cry to the Jewish people (I Maccabees 2:27)

The Miracle of the Oil

When the Maccabees entered the liberated Temple in 165 BCE, they found it in a state of deep desecration. They immediately set about cleansing and rededicating it. For the Temple's great Menorah (the seven-branched candelabra) to be lit, the oil had to be ritually pure and sealed with the High Priest's stamp. They searched the entire Temple and found only one small cruse of undefiled oil — enough to last a single day. Miraculously, this oil burned for eight full days — precisely the time needed to prepare a fresh supply of pure oil. The Talmud (Shabbat 21b) records this miracle and states: "The rabbis taught: On the 25th of Kislev, eight days of Hanukkah begin, on which one may not eulogize or fast. When the Greeks entered the Temple, they defiled all the oils there, and when the Hasmonean dynasty prevailed and defeated them, they searched and found only one flask of oil sealed with the stamp of the High Priest. It contained only enough oil to burn for one day. A miracle was performed with it and it burned for eight days."

Why Eight Days?

The miracle lasted eight days because it takes seven days to press fresh olive oil and one additional day to carry it to Jerusalem — eight days total. During those eight days, the small jar of oil that should have lasted only one day continued to burn, allowing the Temple service to be restored fully. The eight days of Hanukkah commemorate and celebrate this miracle.

Traditions

Customs & Traditions

מִנְהָגֵי חֲנֻכָּה

1. Lighting the Hanukkiah

The central mitzvah of Hanukkah is lighting the Hanukkiah (also called the Hanukkah menorah), a special candelabra with eight branches plus a ninth branch for the shamash (helper candle). Each night, one additional light is added from right to left, but lit from left to right (so the newest night is honored first). The Hanukkiah is traditionally placed in a window or doorway to publicize the miracle — pirsumei nissa — so that all who pass by can see it.

The lights of the Hanukkiah are holy and may not be used for any practical purpose (reading, working, etc.) — they exist solely to proclaim the miracle. The shamash candle is lit first and serves as the "working" light so that the holy Hanukkah lights remain untouched for their sacred purpose.

2. Eating Oil-Fried Foods

In commemoration of the miracle of the oil, it is customary to eat foods fried in oil. The most iconic Hanukkah foods are latkes (potato pancakes) in Ashkenazi tradition, and sufganiyot (jelly-filled doughnuts) which have become popular across all communities. Dairy foods are also eaten in Sephardic communities, in honor of Judith's bravery.

3. Playing Dreidel

A dreidel (Yiddish: dreydl; Hebrew: sevivon) is a four-sided spinning top with Hebrew letters: Nun (נ), Gimel (ג), Hey (ה), Shin (ש) — standing for Nes Gadol Hayah Sham, "A Great Miracle Happened There." In Israel, the Shin is replaced with a Peh (פ) for Poh, "Here." According to legend, when the Greeks forbade Torah study, Jews would gather secretly to study — and when soldiers approached, they would hide their books and pretend to be playing with spinning tops.

4. Hanukkah Gelt

It became customary to give children gelt (money; Yiddish for "gold") on Hanukkah, both real coins and chocolate coins wrapped in gold foil. This tradition honors the newly independent Hasmonean state, which began minting its own coins — a symbol of Jewish sovereignty and freedom. Today, giving gifts has also become associated with Hanukkah.

Prayers & Blessings

Liturgy

Hanukkah Blessings

בְּרָכוֹת חֲנֻכָּה

Before lighting the Hanukkiah each night, three blessings are recited on the first night, and two blessings on all subsequent nights (Shehecheyanu is omitted after the first night).

1. L'hadlik Ner — To Kindle the Light

Blessing for Lighting the Hanukkah Candles

Recited each night before lighting. This is the primary mitzvah blessing for kindling the Hanukkah lights.

בָּרוּךְ אַתָּה יְיָ אֱלֹהֵינוּ מֶלֶךְ הָעוֹלָם, אֲשֶׁר קִדְּשָׁנוּ בְּמִצְוֹתָיו וְצִוָּנוּ לְהַדְלִיק נֵר שֶׁל חֲנֻכָּה.

Baruch atah, Adonai Eloheinu, Melech haolam, asher kid'shanu b'mitzvotav v'tzivanu l'hadlik ner shel Hanukkah.

Blessed are You, Adonai our God, Sovereign of all, who has sanctified us with Your commandments and commanded us to kindle the Hanukkah light.

2. She'asah Nisim — Who Performed Miracles

Blessing of Gratitude for the Miracles

Recited each night, thanking God for the miracles performed for our ancestors in those days.

בָּרוּךְ אַתָּה יְיָ אֱלֹהֵינוּ מֶלֶךְ הָעוֹלָם, שֶׁעָשָׂה נִסִּים לַאֲבוֹתֵינוּ בַּיָּמִים הָהֵם בַּזְּמַן הַזֶּה.

Baruch atah, Adonai Eloheinu, Melech haolam, she'asah nisim la'avoteinu bayamim hahem baz'man hazeh.

Blessed are You, Adonai our God, Sovereign of all, who performed miracles for our ancestors in those days at this season.

3. Shehecheyanu — Blessing of Firsts (First Night Only)

Shehecheyanu

Recited only on the first night, thanking God for sustaining us to reach this joyful season once again.

בָּרוּךְ אַתָּה יְיָ אֱלֹהֵינוּ מֶלֶךְ הָעוֹלָם, שֶׁהֶחֱיָנוּ וְקִיְּמָנוּ וְהִגִּיעָנוּ לַזְּמַן הַזֶּה.

Baruch atah, Adonai Eloheinu, Melech haolam, shehecheyanu v'kiy'manu v'higiyanu laz'man hazeh.

Blessed are You, Adonai our God, Sovereign of all, who has kept us alive, sustained us, and brought us to this season.

Haneirot Halalu — After Lighting

Haneirot Halalu

Recited or sung immediately after lighting the candles, declaring the sacred purpose of the Hanukkah lights.

הַנֵּרוֹת הַלָּלוּ אֲנַחְנוּ מַדְלִיקִין עַל הַנִּסִּים וְעַל הַנִּפְלָאוֹת וְעַל הַתְּשׁוּעוֹת וְעַל הַמִּלְחָמוֹת שֶׁעָשִׂיתָ לַאֲבוֹתֵינוּ בַּיָּמִים הָהֵם בַּזְּמַן הַזֶּה, עַל יְדֵי כֹּהֲנֶיךָ הַקְּדוֹשִׁים. וְכָל שְׁמוֹנַת יְמֵי חֲנֻכָּה הַנֵּרוֹת הַלָּלוּ קֹדֶשׁ הֵם, וְאֵין לָנוּ רְשׁוּת לְהִשְׁתַּמֵּשׁ בָּהֶם אֶלָּא לִרְאוֹתָם בִּלְבָד, כְּדֵי לְהוֹדוֹת וּלְהַלֵּל לְשִׁמְךָ הַגָּדוֹל עַל נִסֶּיךָ וְעַל נִפְלְאוֹתֶיךָ וְעַל יְשׁוּעָתֶךָ.

Haneirot halalu anachnu madlikin al hanisim v'al haniflaot v'al hat'shuot v'al hamilchamot she'asita la'avoteinu bayamim hahem baz'man hazeh al y'dei Kohanecha haK'doshim. V'chol sh'monat y'mei Hanukkah haneirot halalu kodesh hem, v'ein lanu r'shut l'hishtameish bahem ela lir'otam bilvad, k'dei l'hodot ul'hallel l'shimcha hagadol al nisecha v'al niflaotecha v'al y'shuatecha.

We kindle these lights for the miracles and the wonders, for the redemption and the battles that You performed for our forefathers in those days at this season through Your holy priests. During all eight days of Hanukkah these lights are sacred, and we are not permitted to make use of them, but only to look at them, in order to express thanks and praise to Your great Name for Your miracles, Your wonders, and Your salvations.

Hanukkah Songs

Songs of Hanukkah

שִׁירֵי חֲנֻכָּה

These beloved songs are sung around the Hanukkiah — celebrating the miracle, the Maccabees, and the enduring light of the Jewish people.

Maoz Tzur — Rock of Ages

A 13th-century piyyut (liturgical poem) sung after lighting the Hanukkiah. Six stanzas describe the Temple's rededication and various national redemptions. In Ashkenazi communities the first stanza is most commonly sung.

Stanza 1 — Intro / Temple Dedication

תִּכּוֹן בֵּית תְּפִלָּתִי, וְשָׁם תּוֹדָה נְזַבֵּחַ.
לְעֵת תָּכִין מַטְבֵּחַ, מִצָּר הַמְנַבֵּחַ,
אָז אֶגְמוֹר בְּשִׁיר מִזְמוֹר חֲנֻכַּת הַמִּזְבֵּחַ.

Maoz tzur y'shuati, l'cha na'eh l'shabeiach,
Tikon beit t'filati, v'sham todah n'zabeach.
L'eit tachin matbeach, mitzar hamnabeach,
Az egmor b'shir mizmor chanukat hamizbeach.

O mighty stronghold of my salvation, to praise You is a delight.
Restore my House of Prayer and there we will bring a thanksgiving offering.
When You will have prepared the slaughter for the blaspheming foe,
Then I shall complete with a song of hymn the dedication of the altar.

Stanza 2 — Egyptian Exile

רָעוֹת שָׂבְעָה נַפְשִׁי, בְּיָגוֹן כֹּחִי כָּלָה,
חַיַּי מֵרְרוּ בְּקֹשִׁי, בְּשִׁעְבּוּד מַלְכוּת עֶגְלָה.
וּבְיָדוֹ הַגְּדוֹלָה הוֹצִיא אֶת הַסְּגֻלָּה,
חֵיל פַּרְעֹה וְכָל זַרְעוֹ יָרְדוּ כְּאֶבֶן בִּמְצוּלָה.

Ra'ot sav'ah nafshi, b'yagon kochi kalah,
Chayai mer'ru v'koshi, b'shiv'ud malchut eglah.
Uv'yado hag'dolah hotzi et has'gulah,
Cheil Par'oh v'chol zar'o yar'du k'even bim'tzulah.

My soul was sated with troubles, my strength was spent in grief,
My life had grown bitter through toil in slavery under Egypt's reign.
But with His great power He brought out His treasured people,
While Pharaoh's army and all his descendants sank like a stone in the deep.

Stanza 3 — Babylonian Exile

דְּבִיר קָדְשׁוֹ הֱבִיאַנִי, וְגַם שָׁם לֹא שָׁקַטְתִּי,
וּבָא נוֹגֵשׂ וְהִגְלַנִי, כִּי זָרִים עָבַדְתִּי.
וְיֵין רַעַל מָסַכְתִּי כִּמְעַט שֶׁעָבַרְתִּי,
קֵץ בָּבֶל זְרֻבָּבֶל לְקֵץ שִׁבְעִים נוֹשַׁעְתִּי.

D'vir kodsho hevianni, v'gam sham lo shakateti,
Uva noges v'higlani, ki zarim avadeti.
V'yein ra'al masacheti kim'at she'avaret,
Keitz Bavel Z'rubavel l'keitz shiv'im noshaveti.

He brought me to His holy sanctuary, but even there I found no rest,
An oppressor came and exiled me for I had served foreign gods.
I had drunk poisonous wine; I had nearly perished,
But at the end of seventy years, Zerubbabel came and Babylon's end brought my salvation.

Stanza 4 — Persian Exile / Haman

כְּרוֹת קוֹמַת בְּרוֹשׁ בִּקֵּשׁ, אֲגָגִי בֶּן הַמְּדָתָא,

רֹאשׁ יְמִינִי נִשֵּׂאתָ, וְאוֹיֵב שְׁמוֹ מָחִיתָ,
רֹב בָּנָיו וְקִנְיָנָיו עַל הָעֵץ תָּלִיתָ.

K'rot komat b'rosh bikeish, Agagi ben Hamedatah,
V'nihy'tah lo l'fach ulmokeish, v'ga'avato nishbatah.
Rosh Yemini nise'ata, v'oyev sh'mo machita,
Rov banav v'kinyanov al ha'eitz talita.

The Agagite Haman, son of Hammedatha, sought to cut down the tallest cedar,
But it became a trap and snare to him and his arrogance was silenced.
You lifted the head of the Benjaminite [Mordechai], and erased the enemy's name,
And his many sons and possessions You hanged upon the gallows.

Stanza 5 — Greek Oppression / Hanukkah

יְוָנִים נִקְבְּצוּ עָלַי, אֲזַי בִּימֵי חַשְׁמַנִּים,
וּפָרְצוּ חוֹמוֹת מִגְדָּלַי, וְטִמְּאוּ כָּל הַשְּׁמָנִים.
וּמִנּוֹתַר קַנְקַנִּים, נַעֲשָׂה נֵס לַשּׁוֹשַׁנִּים,
בְּנֵי בִינָה יְמֵי שְׁמוֹנָה קָבְעוּ שִׁיר וּרְנָנִים.

Y'vanim nikb'tzu alai, azai bimei Chashmanim,
Ufartz'u chomot migdalai, v'tim'u kol hash'manim.
Uminotar kankanim, na'asah nes lashoashanim,
B'nei vinah y'mei sh'monah kav'u shir ur'nanim.

Greeks gathered against me in the days of the Hasmoneans,
They breached the walls of my towers and defiled all the oils.
But from the remaining flasks a miracle was wrought for the roses [Israel],
And the sages of insight established eight days of song and jubilation.

Stanza 6 — Prayer for Future Redemption

חֲשׂוֹף זְרוֹעַ קָדְשֶׁךָ, וְקָרֵב קֵץ הַיְשׁוּעָה,

כִּי אָרְכָה הַיָּשׁוּעָה, וְאֵין קֵץ לִימֵי הָרָעָה,
דְּחֵה אַדְמוֹן בְּצֵל צַלְמוֹן, הָקֵם לָנוּ רוֹעִים שִׁבְעָה.

Chasof z'roa kodsh'cha, v'karev keitz hay'shuah,
N'kom nikmat avadecha, me'umah harsha'ah.
Ki ar'chah hay'shuah, v'ein keitz liyimei hara'ah,
D'chei Admon b'tzel tzalmon, hakem lanu ro'im shiv'ah.

Bare Your holy arm and hasten the time of salvation,
Avenge the vengeance of Your servants from the wicked nation.
For salvation has been long in coming and there is no end to days of evil,
Repel the Red One [Edom/Rome] in the shadow of the cross, raise up for us seven shepherds.

I Have a Little Dreidel

A beloved Yiddish-origin children's Hanukkah song, popularized in the United States.

I have a little dreidel,
I made it out of clay,
And when it's dry and ready,
Then dreidel I shall play!

Oh, dreidel, dreidel, dreidel,
I made it out of clay,
Oh, dreidel, dreidel, dreidel,
Now dreidel I shall play!

It has a lovely body,
With legs so short and thin,
And when it gets all tired,
It drops and then I win!

Oh, dreidel, dreidel, dreidel,
With legs so short and thin,
Oh, dreidel, dreidel, dreidel,
It drops and then I win!

My dreidel's always playful,
It loves to dance and spin,
A happy game of dreidel,
Come play now, let's begin!

Oh, dreidel, dreidel, dreidel,
It loves to dance and spin,
Oh, dreidel, dreidel, dreidel,
Come play now, let's begin!

I have a little dreidel,
I made it out of gold,
It spins for eight whole evenings,
A story to be told!

Oh, dreidel, dreidel, dreidel,
I made it out of gold,
Oh, dreidel, dreidel, dreidel,
A story to be told!
